About

This year was eventful. After four and a half years on my bachelor's, the plan for my master's was simpler: finish on time, and ideally cum laude. I am about halfway there. The study goals have gone well. The life goals are harder, though not out of reach, and I have made my peace with the fact that they are an ongoing process of focus, refocus, and reconciling rather than something you solve once.

Right now I am wrapping up my thesis and my work at ELIX, where over three years I have moved from monitoring solar installations, to coordinating repairs and leading service teams, to asset-managing a portfolio of more than 250 MW across 200+ sites and 150 partners, and I now lead its expansion into the Spanish market. I am closing out almost twenty years of education with, honestly, still no settled idea of what I want to become. What I do have is theoretical knowledge, several years of real practical experience, and a clear sense of the things I dislike, which turns out to be just as valuable. And before the grown-up career starts, I am going to Southeast Asia: to visit family, get my diving licence, and find places and food I have never known.

1. The through-line

opening

For a long time I assumed I would end up a banker or a stockbroker. Writing my odyssey plan in October, I already suspected that picture was not really me, and I said as much: that I might chase a corporate job out of status or fear of missing out, against my own grain. A year on, I am surer. The tension I keep circling is this: I have come to believe, and I find it hard to accept, that money and status matter less than being happy, and yet I still cannot feel content with simply having or being enough. That paradox is the spine of this portfolio. What follows is not a resolved story. It is the account of someone working it out.

2. Who I am, and who I am not

covers: self-insight

When I was younger I was sure I wanted to be a banker or a stockbroker. I no longer think that picture is who I am, though not for the reason I might once have given. Three years of asset management at ELIX have shown me I am genuinely comfortable with analytical work: performance data at scale, modelling, the quantitative side of running a portfolio. I can do it, and do it well. What I have learned is that being capable of something is not the same as being driven by it. The analysis is a tool I am glad to own; it is not where my energy lives. I am a connector and an organiser first, and what pulls me is activating people, shaping how a group works, and making things move.

The international internship I set as my main goal in October did not happen the way I planned. The arrangement fell through on the company's side rather than mine. That was discouraging, but it pushed me to ask what I actually wanted from the experience in the first place.

The clearest lesson of the year is that I am not built for the cubicle, and I have made peace with that. I would far rather carry a heavy workload and keep a real life around it than hold a high-status title that costs eighty-hour weeks and the habits people lean on to survive them. For a long time I would have taken the status anyway. I still find it hard to let go of, even though I have come to believe it matters less than being happy.

3. How I work, and where I step back

covers: metacognition

In group work this year I settled into a clear pattern. I take the lead on structure: I divide the tasks, chair the meetings, and set the direction. Once the discussion turns to the content itself and how to operationalise the work, I deliberately pull back and let others run with it, stepping in only to steer when it drifts. With the group I worked with for most of the year, this worked, and I think I kept everyone activated. The stepping back is a choice, not a retreat: I trust the people around me to own the detail, and I would rather hold the structure together and then pick up whatever task falls through the gaps than insist on driving the content myself.

I do not always get this right. On one project I was on holiday for a week and could not do my part. I had communicated it, but not as well or as early as I should have, which left the others to absorb more than was fair. Looking back, the failure was not the absence, which was reasonable, but the communication around it: telling a team you will be unavailable is not the same as telling them in good time, and in enough detail, for them to plan around it. Naming that gap honestly is the first step to not repeating it.

4. Beyond the job

covers: broad horizon

My interests reach past my own career. I care about climate, specifically about cutting carbon and greenhouse gas emissions, and about connecting people with food. These are not abstractions I reach for to sound responsible; they are the directions I actually want my work to push in. Frameworks like the UN Sustainable Development Goals and the EU's ESG and sustainability directives put words to it: these are goals worth building a career around.

My work at ELIX is where this stops being a value and becomes the job. Developing solar and battery-storage projects is clean energy with real commercial substance, which is exactly the intersection I am looking for: the part of me that wants to earn well and the part that wants the work to matter do not have to fight there. The line I want to hold is simple to state: make money while leaving nobody worse off in the process. I know that is more an ideal than a guarantee, since plenty of profitable work does leave someone worse off. Part of why clean energy convinces me is that building generation which displaces higher-emission alternatives comes about as close to a positive-sum bet as commercial work gets, even if it is never perfectly clean.

5. Where it stands now

close

So the tension is not resolved, and I am not going to pretend it is. What I do have is a deliberate next step. From September I am taking three months in Southeast Asia, partly to see family, partly to learn to dive, partly to find food and places I have never known. After that I will join a firm for at least a year. If the work is genuinely useful and I enjoy it, I will stay and build on it. If it is not, a traineeship is still on the table. Skipping this round of traineeships to travel first is a choice, not something I am drifting into. I would rather begin the next thing on my own terms.

Learning Plan

My SMARTO learning plan, submitted October 2025. Reproduced as written, it is the baseline this reflection measures against, including the line about being "less analytical" that the year since has revised.

  1. The goal is to secure an international internship before the first of February. It might seem unrealistic or unreachable, but for me, that is precisely the point. Wanting the hard way, not the easy way. I want to not only broaden my horizons professionally but also live in another country and experience their (work) culture.
  2. When someone asks the question; What field do you want to work in? What are your strengths? What is your 5-year plan? And what is the motivation behind your answers? I want to be able to answer these. This might be an ongoing process for the rest of my life; however, it is beneficial to continually develop new objectives based on my changing perspectives. There are no obstacles but my own time and effort for thinking about this.
  3. At the end of my studies, I would like to be in either a corporate job or an entrepreneurial setting, like a start-up or scale-up. This corporate job may not suit me, but I might take it out of a desire for status or fear of missing out. But I might be convinced that through the coming year, I will be able to come at ease with the fact that the world does not suit me. I am more creative, less analytical and more risk-taking than the cubicle world. a. Time will tell how this works out.
